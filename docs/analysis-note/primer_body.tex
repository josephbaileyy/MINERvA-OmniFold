\section{What was measured, and why it is hard}

A neutrino is a nearly invisible particle: it passes through ordinary matter
almost without a trace. To study neutrinos, the MINERvA experiment at Fermilab
fired an intense beam of them at a finely segmented detector and recorded the
rare occasions when one struck a nucleus and produced visible particles --- in
this analysis, an interaction that creates a muon (a heavy cousin of the
electron). The quantity of interest is the \emph{cross section}: essentially the
probability that such an interaction happens, measured as a function of the
muon's motion --- how fast it travels along the beam ($\pPar$) and how much it is
deflected sideways ($\pT$).

The difficulty is that the detector never measures these quantities perfectly.
A muon's recorded momentum is a blurred version of its true momentum, the blur
being the detector's finite resolution and efficiency. The job of
\emph{unfolding} is to remove that blur: to recover the true distribution that
nature produced from the smeared distribution the detector recorded. It is the
same problem as deblurring a photograph, and like deblurring it is delicate ---
done carelessly it amplifies noise into artifacts.

\section{The method, in plain language}

The traditional way to unfold builds a big table --- a ``migration matrix'' ---
that records, for every bin of true momentum, how events leak into neighbouring
bins of measured momentum, and then tries to invert that leakage. It works, but
it has a crippling limitation: each new quantity you want to measure multiplies
the number of bins, and the table quickly becomes too large and too sparse to be
useful. In practice this caps the method at one or two quantities at a time.

This analysis uses a different, machine-learning method called \textbf{OmniFold}.
Instead of a table of bins, it trains a computer to compare the real data with a
detailed computer simulation, event by event, and to learn a \emph{weight} for
each simulated event --- a number that says ``there should be a little more (or
less) of this kind of event to make the simulation look like the data.'' Because
the simulation knows both the true and the measured version of every event, that
weight can be carried back from the measured world to the true world. Repeating
the comparison a few times morphs the simulation until it matches the data, and
the morphed \emph{true} simulation is the unfolded answer.

The decisive advantage is what happens when you want to measure more quantities
at once. In the table method, adding a quantity multiplies the bins. In
OmniFold, adding a quantity is just handing the computer one more column of
numbers to learn from --- the cost barely grows. That single property is what
makes everything in the next section possible.

\section{What we found}

\subsection*{First, a trust check: reproduce a known result}

Before trusting a new method, one must show it reproduces an established
measurement. MINERvA already published this muon cross section using the
traditional method. Our machine-learning result agrees with it closely: the
overall interaction rate matches to about one percent (ratio $\ratioTot$), and
\SI{\binsTen}{\percent} of the individual measurement cells agree within ten
percent. Just as important, our \emph{uncertainties} --- worked out completely
independently --- come out essentially the same size as the published ones
(\SI{\uqMedian}{\percent} versus \SI{\uqPaper}{\percent} typical). The new method
reproduces the old answer and its error bars.

\subsection*{Then, measure more at once --- and rediscover known physics}

With the trust check passed, we used OmniFold's defining ability and measured
several quantities \emph{simultaneously} for the first time at MINERvA: the muon
momentum together with the available energy ($\Eavail$, roughly how much energy
the struck nucleus released), the momentum transfer ($q_{3}$), and the hadronic
mass ($W$). Each richer measurement, when collapsed back down, reproduces the
simpler one --- a built-in consistency check.

The first physics payoff appears in the available energy. When we compare the
data to four independent state-of-the-art simulations, all four fall short of
the data in the low-energy region (Fig.~\ref{fig:primergen}). This is not a
surprise --- it is a known nuclear-physics effect, in which a neutrino
occasionally kicks \emph{two} nucleons at once (``2p2h''), that simulations have
historically struggled to model. The significance is that our completely
independent method rediscovers it: we checked that minor nuclear-physics knobs
cannot account for the gap (they move the result by under one percent), whereas
adding the missing two-nucleon process fills it in, exactly as expected. And
where MINERvA has a dedicated published measurement of this region, ours agrees
with it at central-value level on the maximal common grid; the former
covariance-based consistency metric is withheld pending corrected production.

\subsection*{A new puzzle that no simulation explains}

Adding the hadronic-mass axis $W$ lets us look where the simulations still miss
\emph{after} the two-nucleon process is included. The leftover discrepancy sits
in a specific corner: high available energy \emph{and} high $W$ --- the
``deep-inelastic'' region where the neutrino shatters the nucleon. There, all
four simulations undershoot the central result. The exact significance is
withheld while the corrected covariance is regenerated. This is the kind of localised discrepancy that
points theorists toward what their models are missing --- and it was only
visible because the unbinned method let us slice the data this finely.

\subsection*{Two steps toward the future}

Two further results show where the method is heading. First, we ran a more
ambitious version of OmniFold (a ``point-cloud transformer'') that learns
directly from the \emph{raw} detector signals rather than from hand-engineered
summaries, and it reproduces the standard result --- a proof of principle for
unfolding straight from low-level data. Second, the published measurement throws
away about a third of the interactions because the muon flies at too steep an
angle or too slowly for the standard analysis. Because OmniFold corrects for
lost events naturally, we were able to relax those cuts and report a first
\emph{full-phase-space} cross section that keeps the discarded third
($\sigma_{\mathrm{FPS}}=\SI{\fpsSigma}{cm^2/nucleon}$,
\SI{\fpsAbove}{\percent} larger), while being honest, cell by cell, about how
much of the extrapolation rests on the simulation.

\section{The takeaway}

Unbinned machine-learning unfolding reproduces a flagship MINERvA measurement
\emph{and} does what the traditional method cannot: measure many quantities at
once. Using that, this analysis independently confirmed a known nuclear-physics
effect, exposed a new discrepancy that no current simulation explains, and
produced a cross section that no longer discards a third of the data. The full
technical record --- every number, uncertainty, and cross-check --- is in the
companion analysis note.
